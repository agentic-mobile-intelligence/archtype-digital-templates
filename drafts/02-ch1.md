# Chapter 1: What Makes a Good Template

Before we can talk about how templates drift, fork, and resync, we need a
precise working definition of what a template actually is. The word is
overloaded. A JavaScript developer, a Rails developer, and a platform engineer
will each picture something different when they hear it, and those pictures are
not compatible. A good chunk of the pain downstream of any template ecosystem
starts with this ambiguity: the people producing the template and the people
consuming it are often working from different mental models.

This chapter establishes the vocabulary for the rest of the book. We will
distinguish templates from their nearby cousins, draw the line between the parts
of a template that should stay stable and the parts that are expected to move,
and name the single design constraint that, in my experience, separates
templates that age well from templates that rot.

Throughout the chapter I will use a concrete example:
`template-start-from-here`, a minimal repository that exists for one purpose —
to be forked. It ships a `publishing/` directory containing build tooling,
linters, and a layout specification, and a `drafts/` directory where the
instance author writes the actual content. Every idea in this book can be
demonstrated on that skeleton, so when the abstraction gets fuzzy, picture
that repo.

## Template vs Scaffold vs Boilerplate

These three words are often used interchangeably, and that is where the trouble
starts. They describe different commitments between the author and the
consumer, and conflating them leads to predictable disappointments.

A **boilerplate** is a starting pile of code you copy and then own outright.
Once you have cloned it, the relationship is severed. If the boilerplate's
author publishes a new version next month, that is their problem, not yours.
Boilerplates optimize for a frictionless first commit and nothing else. They
are not wrong — they are appropriate when the consumer genuinely wants a clean
break from the source — but they make no promises about the future.

A **scaffold** is a one-shot generator. You run it, it produces files, and the
generator itself is not meant to be present in the output. Rails'
`rails new`, `create-react-app`, and `cookiecutter` are scaffolds. The
generator encodes opinions at the moment of generation, bakes them into the
output, and then leaves. There is no upstream to sync with because there is no
persistent link between the generated project and the generator. Scaffolds are
excellent for bootstrapping and terrible for evolution — any improvement to the
scaffold only benefits projects generated after the improvement lands.

A **template**, in the sense this book uses the word, is neither of those. It
is a repository that is intended to be forked *and then stay forked*, with the
expectation that changes will flow in both directions over time. The upstream
continues to live. The downstream instance continues to live. Both will evolve,
and the relationship between them is the object of study. When I use the word
"template" in the rest of this book, I mean specifically this third thing: a
living, bidirectional artifact.

`template-start-from-here` is a template in this strict sense. When you fork
it, you are not expected to sever ties. The `publishing/` directory is going to
improve upstream — new linters, better CI, corrected layout rules — and those
improvements are meant to reach you. Conversely, when you discover a better way
to structure the build pipeline while using your fork, that improvement should
be able to travel back.

## Stable Tooling, Unstable Content

The single most important design move in a good template is the separation of
the parts that the upstream owns from the parts that the downstream owns. In
`template-start-from-here`, that separation is physical: two top-level
directories, each with a clearly assigned owner.

The `publishing/` directory contains the tooling. This is the build system,
the layout rules, the export scripts, the linters, the CI config, the outline
schema. It is stable in the sense that the consumer does not modify it. When
`publishing/` changes, the change came from upstream, and the consumer's job
is to accept that change cleanly — not to rewrite it, not to diverge from it,
not to "just tweak this one thing." The moment a downstream instance starts
editing files under `publishing/`, the sync story begins to collapse, because
every future upstream change becomes a potential merge conflict.

The `drafts/` directory contains the content. This is the manuscript, the
chapters, the author's actual work. It is unstable in the sense that it
changes constantly, and it is local in the sense that the upstream has no
opinion about what goes in it. Upstream ships `drafts/` with placeholder or
example files, but those are meant to be deleted or rewritten on day one of
the instance's life.

This partition — stable tooling, unstable content — is the load-bearing
convention of the whole system. It is what makes the rest of the patterns in
this book possible. Drift detection works because we can tell tooling changes
from content changes by looking at the path. Upstream pinning is tolerable
because the pinned surface is bounded. The backport pattern works because
content and tooling flow in opposite directions and we know which is which.

A template that mixes tooling and content in the same directories can still
be useful, but it cannot be synced. Every upgrade becomes manual archaeology.
The directory boundary is not cosmetic; it is the contract.

## Designing for Downstream Adoption

The final question — and the one that separates templates that thrive from
templates that become abandonware — is whether the template was designed with
the downstream consumer's experience in mind.

Most templates are designed from the upstream's perspective. The author builds
what they wanted when they were solving the problem the first time, commits
it, and publishes the repo. This is how most templates start, and it is fine
for a v0. It stops being fine the moment a second consumer shows up, because
the template now needs to survive encounters with use cases its author did
not anticipate.

Designing for downstream adoption means a few concrete things. First, the
default state of the template, as forked, must be runnable — not "runnable
after you fill in these three environment variables and rename the placeholder
strings," but actually runnable. A consumer who cannot get a green build in
the first ten minutes will not come back. `template-start-from-here` achieves
this by shipping a trivial but real `drafts/` payload that the `publishing/`
tooling can successfully process end-to-end.

Second, the places a consumer is expected to edit must be obvious, and the
places they are not expected to edit must be equally obvious. The
`publishing/` and `drafts/` split is one signal; another is that every file
the consumer is meant to customize sits at a predictable path with an
unambiguous name. There should be no hunting.

Third — and this is the subtlest point — the template should be designed so
that the consumer's future self, six months in, can still tell which parts of
their repo came from upstream and which parts they wrote. If the boundary is
clear on day one but muddy on day 180, the template has failed at its actual
job. This is why the directory-level separation matters more than any README
could. A convention enforced by filesystem layout survives; a convention
enforced by prose in a `CONTRIBUTING.md` does not.

These three properties — runnable on fork, obvious edit surface, legible
provenance — are the practical definition of a well-designed template. The
remaining chapters of Part 1 build on this definition: Chapter 2 traces what
happens to an instance over time, and Chapter 3 formalizes the upstream and
downstream relationship that makes this whole enterprise work.
