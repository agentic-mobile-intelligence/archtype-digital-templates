# Chapter 4: Upstream Pinning

Every template instance begins life as a snapshot. The moment you generate a project from a template, you capture the template's state at that point in time --- its directory structure, its build configuration, its CI pipeline, its dependency versions. From that moment forward, the template keeps moving. New patterns emerge, bugs get fixed, dependencies get bumped. Your instance, meanwhile, drifts in its own direction.

Upstream pinning is the practice of recording exactly which version of the template your instance was created from and deliberately choosing when to absorb changes. It transforms the relationship between template and instance from an implicit, decaying connection into an explicit, managed one.

Without a pin, you have no anchor. You cannot answer the question "how far behind am I?" because you have no reference point. You cannot merge upstream changes because you do not know which changes you have already absorbed and which are new. Pinning gives you that anchor.

## Tracking a Specific Upstream Ref

The simplest form of upstream pinning is recording a commit SHA. Somewhere in your instance --- a dotfile, a field in your project manifest, a dedicated configuration file --- you write down the exact commit of the template repository that your instance reflects.

A common convention is a `.template-version` file at the root of the instance:

```
template: archetype-digital-templates
ref: a1b2c3d4e5f6789012345678
upstream: https://github.com/org/archetype-digital-templates
pinned_at: 2026-03-15
```

This file does not enforce anything on its own. Its power is entirely in the clarity it provides. When you open a project six months from now and wonder whether it includes the CI fix your platform team shipped in February, you check the pin. You compare the pinned ref against the template's commit history. The answer is unambiguous.

Some teams prefer to pin to a tag rather than a raw SHA. Tags are more human-readable --- `v2.4.1` tells you more at a glance than a forty-character hash. But tags can be moved or deleted, and they carry an implicit promise of semantic meaning that may or may not hold. A SHA is immutable and precise. When in doubt, pin to SHAs and use tags as supplementary metadata.

Branch-based pinning --- recording that your instance tracks `main` or `stable` --- is the weakest form of pin. It tells you which stream of changes you intend to follow, but it does not tell you where you actually are. A branch pin without a commit ref is like saying "I follow the news" without being able to name the last headline you read.

The pin should be committed to version control alongside everything else in the instance. It is part of the project's state. When someone opens a pull request that updates the pin, the diff is visible: "we moved from ref `a1b2c3d` to ref `e4f5g6h`." That diff becomes a natural place for discussion about what changed upstream, whether the team is ready to absorb those changes, and what testing is needed.

For organizations managing many instances of the same template, the pin also enables fleet-wide visibility. A simple script can scan all repositories for their `.template-version` files and produce a report showing which instances are current, which are a few versions behind, and which have fallen so far behind that the upgrade path is nontrivial. Chapter 10 explores this fleet perspective in detail.

## Intentional Upgrade Ceremonies

Pinning is only half the pattern. The other half is the upgrade ceremony --- the deliberate, scheduled process of moving your pin forward.

The word "ceremony" is chosen carefully. Upgrading your upstream pin should not be something that happens accidentally, as a side effect of another change, or in a rushed Friday afternoon commit. It should be a distinct event with its own pull request, its own review, and its own testing.

A typical upgrade ceremony follows this sequence:

**1. Review the upstream changelog.** Before touching any code, read what changed in the template between your current pin and the version you intend to upgrade to. If the template repository follows the practices described in Chapter 9, this changelog should clearly distinguish breaking changes from non-breaking ones. If it does not, you will need to read the commit history directly.

**2. Create a dedicated branch.** The upgrade gets its own branch, not tangled with feature work. Name it something explicit: `template-upgrade/v2.4-to-v2.5` or `upstream-sync/2026-04`.

**3. Apply the upstream changes.** How you do this depends on your tooling. Some teams use `git merge` with the template repository configured as a remote. Others use patch files. Still others use a sync tool that understands the template's structure and can apply changes selectively. The mechanics matter less than the principle: changes from upstream arrive as a discrete, reviewable unit.

**4. Resolve conflicts.** This is where downstream customization meets upstream evolution. Conflicts are not bugs --- they are information. Every conflict tells you about a place where your instance has diverged from the template in a way that intersects with something the template maintainers changed. Some conflicts are trivial. Others reveal that your customization and the template's direction are fundamentally pulling apart, requiring a design decision rather than a mechanical merge resolution.

**5. Run the full test suite.** Not the quick smoke tests. The full suite. Template upgrades can introduce subtle changes --- a different default timeout, a new linting rule, a changed directory structure --- that pass a cursory check but break something downstream.

**6. Update the pin.** Once the merge is clean and tests pass, update your `.template-version` file to reflect the new ref. This is the last step, not the first. The pin records what you have actually absorbed, not what you intend to absorb.

**7. Merge with a clear commit message.** The commit message for a template upgrade should reference the old and new refs, summarize the key changes absorbed, and note any conflicts that required judgment calls.

The cadence of upgrade ceremonies varies by team and context. Some teams upgrade monthly. Others upgrade quarterly or align upgrades with their own release cycles. The right cadence depends on how actively the template is evolving, how much your instance has diverged, and how much risk your team can absorb in a single upgrade.

What matters is that the cadence is intentional. A team that upgrades "whenever we get around to it" is a team that will eventually face a massive, painful merge when the gap between their pin and the template's current state becomes too wide to ignore.

## When to Stay Behind

Not every upstream change deserves to be adopted, and not every instance should track the latest version. There are legitimate, even wise, reasons to stay behind.

**Stability windows.** If your project is approaching a release, a demo, or a compliance audit, introducing upstream changes adds unnecessary risk. Pin to a known-good version and stay there until your stability window closes. The template will still be there when you are ready.

**Irrelevant changes.** Templates serve multiple consumers. A change that adds support for a deployment target you do not use, or a CI provider you have never adopted, is noise for your instance. You can skip these changes entirely or absorb them selectively. Not every upstream commit is addressed to you.

**Breaking changes you are not ready for.** Sometimes the template introduces a change that is correct and beneficial but requires significant downstream work to absorb. A migration from one build system to another, a restructuring of the directory layout, a major dependency bump --- these changes are worth making, but on your timeline, not the template's. Pin to the last version before the breaking change and schedule the upgrade when your team has capacity.

**Philosophical divergence.** Occasionally, the template moves in a direction that does not align with your project's needs. Perhaps the template adopts a convention your team has deliberately rejected, or adds complexity that your simpler use case does not warrant. In these cases, staying behind is not laziness --- it is a design decision. Document it. Note in your `.template-version` file or your project's decision log why you are intentionally not upgrading past a certain point.

The danger of staying behind is not the staying itself --- it is the forgetting. A team that consciously decides to remain on version 2.3 while the template advances to 3.0 is making a reasonable choice. A team that is on version 2.3 because nobody noticed the template moved to 3.0 is accumulating invisible risk. The difference between the two is documentation and awareness.

This is why drift detection, covered in Chapter 6, pairs so naturally with upstream pinning. The pin tells you where you are. Drift detection tells you how far the world has moved since you last looked. Together, they give you the information you need to make an intentional choice rather than drifting by default.

Staying behind also creates a responsibility: when you do eventually upgrade, you own the accumulated gap. A team that skips three minor versions and then upgrades will face the combined conflicts of all three. This is not a reason to never skip --- it is a reason to make the skip a conscious decision with eyes open about the eventual cost.
